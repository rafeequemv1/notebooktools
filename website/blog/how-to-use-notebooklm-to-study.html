7 Ways to Use NotebookLM to Study Smarter

June 23, 2026 · 8 min read

NotebookLM turns scattered course materials into one queryable study hub. If you are figuring out how to use NotebookLM to study, start with a simple rule: one notebook per course, every source inside it, then use chat to summarize, drill, and connect ideas across readings and lectures.

Most students do not need another app — they need a system. NotebookLM is free, handles PDFs and YouTube natively, and grounds answers in your actual materials. The workflows below are battle-tested patterns for undergrads, grad students, and self-learners. Where imports slow you down, NotebookTools removes the copy-paste step for videos and web pages.

Before you start: set up one notebook per course

Create a dedicated notebook for each class — "BIOL 201 Spring," "Macro Econ," and so on. Add your syllabus first; it becomes the roadmap for every other source. As the semester progresses, drop in lecture slides, assigned readings, recorded lectures, and supplementary articles. The notebook grows with the course, and NotebookLM's answers get richer every week.

  1. 1. Turn lecture videos into searchable notes

    Recorded lectures on YouTube or your school's portal are gold — if you can search them. Add each lecture video as a NotebookLM source, then ask for structured notes: key concepts, definitions, and anything the professor emphasized.

    Workflow: After each class, import the lecture video (paste the URL or use NotebookTools on the watch page). Prompt: "Summarize this lecture in a study outline with definitions I need to memorize." For the full import walkthrough, see our guide on summarizing YouTube videos in NotebookLM.

    Why it beats re-watching: You get a searchable summary in minutes and can ask follow-ups — "explain the example from minute 20" — without scrubbing through an hour of video.

  2. 2. Merge slides, textbook chapters, and articles

    The real power of NotebookLM is cross-source synthesis. Upload lecture slides and the matching textbook chapter and the professor's recommended article. Then ask how they align or where they disagree.

    Workflow: Prompt: "Compare the lecture slides with the textbook chapter. What did the lecture add that the book did not cover? What should I prioritize for the exam?" This surfaces gaps before you discover them on test day.

  3. 3. Build exam-week study guides automatically

    Two weeks before the exam, run a batch of prompts across your full notebook. Generate a master outline, a glossary, and practice questions — all grounded in the materials you actually studied.

    Workflow: Use prompts from our NotebookLM prompts guide — especially the study outline, flashcard generator, and practice exam questions. Review the output, then ask NotebookLM to "go deeper on the three topics I marked as weak."

  4. 4. Listen on the go with Audio Overviews

    NotebookLM can generate podcast-style Audio Overviews from your notebook. Commute time becomes review time — hear a conversational recap of the week's material without staring at a screen.

    Workflow: Generate an Audio Overview after each major unit. Listen once for orientation, then a second time while glancing at your outline to connect spoken ideas to written notes. Not a replacement for active study, but excellent for spaced exposure.

  5. 5. Prep for seminars and discussion sections

    Discussion-based courses reward preparation, not just memorization. Use NotebookLM to extract debate-worthy claims, unanswered questions, and connections between assigned readings.

    Workflow: Prompt: "List the three most debatable claims across this week's readings. For each, summarize the evidence and one counterargument a classmate might raise." Walk into section with talking points, not just highlights.

  6. 6. Draft essays with source-backed outlines

    NotebookLM is not a ghostwriter — but it is an excellent outlining partner. Ask for thesis options, argument structures, and evidence maps drawn only from your uploaded sources.

    Workflow: Prompt: "Propose three thesis statements for a paper on [TOPIC], each supported by evidence from my sources. Include which sources back each claim." You write the prose; NotebookLM keeps you honest about what your materials actually say.

  7. 7. Run a weekly review ritual

    Studying with NotebookLM works best as a habit, not a cram session. Every Sunday, spend twenty minutes updating your course notebook and running a quick review prompt.

    Workflow: Add any new slides, readings, or videos from the week. Prompt: "What are the five most important ideas from this week's new sources? How do they connect to what we covered earlier in the semester?" This weekly loop compounds — by finals, your notebook is a complete, queryable record of the course.

Study smarter, not longer

NotebookLM does not replace reading, problem sets, or office hours. It reduces the overhead of organizing and retrieving what you already learned. Combine these seven workflows with active recall (practice questions, flashcards, teaching concepts aloud) and you will retain more in less time.

Common student questions

How many sources can I add?
NotebookLM supports dozens of sources per notebook — enough for a full semester. Prioritize high-value materials over dumping every optional reading.

Will my professor know I use AI?
NotebookLM helps you study your own materials — like a smart index. Always follow your institution's academic integrity policies for graded work.

FAQ

Is NotebookLM free for students?
Yes. Core NotebookLM features are free with a Google account.

Can I share a study notebook with classmates?
NotebookLM supports notebook sharing. Coordinate with your study group so everyone benefits from the same source set.

What should I import first?
Syllabus, then lecture slides and assigned readings. Add videos as they become available.
