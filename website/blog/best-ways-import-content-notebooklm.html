6 best ways to import content into NotebookLM

June 21, 2026 · 8 min read

NotebookLM accepts content from many places — but not every import method is equally fast. Whether you need a YouTube-to-NotebookLM Chrome extension, a simple web importer, or a way to bulk-add PDFs, this guide ranks six approaches so you can pick the right one for your workflow.

Google built NotebookLM as a source-grounded research assistant. The quality of your notebook depends on what you feed it, and how easily you can add material while reading, watching, or reviewing documents. Below we compare six import methods from fastest for everyday browsing to best for archival files — with an eye on the 50-source-per-notebook limit you will want to manage over time.

  1. NotebookTools Chrome extension (best for web, YouTube, and X)

    For most people who research in a browser, NotebookTools is the top choice. It is a free Chrome extension that acts as a dedicated YouTube-to-NotebookLM importer and NotebookLM web importer in one package — no copy-paste, no tab juggling.

    What it does:

    • One-click import from any YouTube watch page
    • Import the webpage you are currently reading
    • Send X (Twitter) posts directly to a notebook
    • Right-click highlighted text → Add to NotebookLM
    • Pick an existing notebook or create a new one from the popup

    Install the extension, stay signed into NotebookLM in Chrome, and click the NotebookLM button wherever you are browsing. The source appears in your notebook within seconds. This is the workflow we recommend for lecture videos, news articles, threads, and any content you discover while researching. See our full YouTube summarization guide for prompt ideas once videos are imported.

  2. Manual URL paste (built-in, no extension needed)

    NotebookLM's native Add source → Website flow works for any public URL. Open your notebook, click Add source, paste the link, and wait for processing. This method supports most articles, blog posts, and YouTube watch URLs.

    The downside is friction. You must copy the URL, switch to NotebookLM, find the right notebook, and paste. For a single source that is fine; for a research session with dozens of tabs it becomes tedious. Manual paste is a reliable fallback when you are on a browser or device where extensions are not available.

  3. Google Drive integration

    If your research lives in Google Workspace, connect Google Drive as a source. NotebookLM can ingest Google Docs, Slides, and other Drive files without downloading and re-uploading. This is ideal for team documents, shared lecture notes, and drafts you are already editing collaboratively.

    Drive import keeps files in sync — when you update a Doc, re-syncing the source refreshes what NotebookLM can reference. Each Drive file counts as one source toward your notebook quota. For large Drive libraries, curate which files you connect rather than importing an entire folder blindly.

  4. Copy-paste text (quick captures and excerpts)

    Sometimes you only need a paragraph, a quote, or notes from a paywalled page you cannot import as a URL. NotebookLM's pasted text source lets you drop raw text directly into a notebook. Each paste block counts as one source.

    Use this for meeting notes, email excerpts, chat transcripts, or sections of documents you cannot upload. NotebookTools complements this with right-click highlight import — select text on any page and send just that passage instead of the full article. Pair pasted notes with full-document sources for richer context.

  5. Upload PDFs and documents

    Academic papers, scanned books, reports, and slide decks often arrive as PDF uploads. Drag a file into Add source or browse your computer to upload. NotebookLM extracts text and makes the document queryable. Supported formats also include .txt, .md, and audio files depending on your region and account settings.

    Remember the ~200 MB file size cap and the per-notebook source limit. Split oversized scans into chapters if needed, and prefer one combined PDF over many tiny fragments when citation granularity is not important. Details in our source limits guide.

  6. Pasted notes and manual summaries

    The sixth method is deliberately low-tech: write or paste your own summary notes as a source. After reading an article or watching a video elsewhere, paste your bullet-point takeaways into NotebookLM. This is useful when the original content cannot be imported — private Slack threads, physical books, or offline conversations.

    Self-authored notes become first-class sources you can cross-reference with PDFs and web pages. The trade-off is that NotebookLM cannot cite back to the original material unless you also import it. Use pasted notes as a supplement, not a replacement, when citations matter.

Which method should you use?

Match the tool to the content type. Browser-based discovery → NotebookTools. Existing Google Docs → Drive. Archival PDFs → upload. Quick excerpts → paste or highlight. Most power users combine all six methods in a single notebook and let NotebookLM synthesize across them.

FAQ

Is NotebookTools affiliated with Google?
No. It is an independent extension that uses your existing NotebookLM session.

Do I need an extension to import YouTube videos?
No — pasting the URL works. The extension just saves time.

Import not working?
Check that you are signed in, the notebook is not full, and the URL is public. See support for troubleshooting.
