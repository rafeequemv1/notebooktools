What Is NotebookLM? A Plain-English Guide

June 23, 2026 · 10 min read

NotebookLM is Google's free AI research notebook. You upload your own documents, links, and videos, then ask questions and get answers grounded in those materials. Think of it as a smart assistant that has read everything you give it and can point you back to the exact source when you need proof.

If you have been hearing people talk about Google NotebookLM and wondering whether it is worth your time, you are in the right place. This guide explains what it actually does, who it helps most, and how to try it in the next ten minutes. No jargon, no hype. Just a clear picture of a tool that has quietly become one of the most useful free AI products Google offers.

What NotebookLM actually is

NotebookLM is an AI-powered notebook made by Google. The "LM" stands for language model, which is the technology that reads and responds to your questions. But unlike a general chatbot, NotebookLM does not try to know everything on the internet. It only knows what you put inside your notebook.

You start by creating a notebook, which is basically a project folder. Inside that folder, you add sources: a PDF of a research paper, a Google Doc with meeting notes, a YouTube lecture, a webpage you saved, or text you pasted from an email. Once your sources are in, you open a chat panel and start asking questions.

The AI reads across all your sources at once. Ask "What are the main arguments in these three articles?" and it pulls together an answer from each one. Ask "Summarize chapter 4" and it works from the PDF you uploaded. Every response includes citations, little numbered references that jump you to the exact passage in your source. That citation feature is the heart of what makes NotebookLM different from other AI tools.

Google also built an optional feature called Audio Overview, which turns your notebook into a podcast-style conversation between two AI hosts. It is a fun way to review material while commuting or doing dishes. But the core experience is simple: your sources in, smart answers out.

Who NotebookLM is for

NotebookLM is not built for one type of person. It fits anyone who works with information they need to understand, organize, or reuse. Here are the groups that tend to get the most value:

Students use it to combine lecture slides, textbook chapters, and YouTube videos into one study hub. Instead of flipping between five tabs during exam week, you ask one notebook to explain concepts, compare readings, or generate practice questions.

Researchers and academics upload papers, grant proposals, and interview transcripts. NotebookLM helps them spot themes across dozens of sources, draft literature review outlines, and pull direct quotes with citations already attached.

Writers and content creators feed in background research, interview notes, and reference articles. They use chat to find connections, check facts against their sources, and outline pieces without losing track of where each idea came from.

Curious people learning something new might load a few articles and a video series on a topic like personal finance, gardening, or history. NotebookLM becomes a patient tutor that only teaches from the materials you chose, not random internet noise.

If your work involves reading, comparing, or summarizing documents, NotebookLM will probably feel immediately useful. If you mostly want open-ended creative writing with no source material, a general chatbot might fit better.

How NotebookLM works, step by step

The workflow is straightforward once you see it once. Here is the full path from zero to your first useful answer.

Step 1: Create a notebook. Go to notebooklm.google.com and sign in with your Google account. Click the button to create a new notebook. Give it a name that matches your project, like "Biology 101" or "Q3 Marketing Research."

Step 2: Add your sources. Click "Add source" and choose how you want to import. You can upload a file, paste a URL, connect a Google Doc, or paste text directly. Add everything relevant to this project. A notebook with three strong sources beats one with thirty random ones.

Step 3: Chat with your materials. Open the chat panel on the right side of the screen. Type a question in plain English. Try "Summarize the key findings" or "What do these sources disagree about?" NotebookLM reads your sources and responds with an answer plus citation numbers you can click to verify.

Step 4: Use follow-up questions. Treat it like a conversation. Ask "Can you explain that more simply?" or "Give me three bullet points I could use in a presentation." Each follow-up stays grounded in your notebook.

Step 5 (optional): Generate an Audio Overview. If you want a spoken recap, click the Audio Overview button. NotebookLM creates a conversational audio summary of your sources. It takes a few minutes to generate, but it is a nice way to review on the go.

That is the whole loop. Create, add, chat, refine. Most people have a working notebook within five minutes of signing in.

What you can add as sources

NotebookLM accepts a solid range of formats. Here is what you can feed it today:

PDFs are the most common source type. Research papers, scanned textbook pages, slide decks exported as PDF, and government reports all work well.

Google Docs connect directly if they live in your Google Drive. Great for notes, drafts, and shared documents your team already uses.

Websites can be added by pasting a URL. NotebookLM fetches the page content and makes it searchable inside your notebook.

YouTube videos work the same way. Paste a video link and NotebookLM processes the transcript so you can ask questions about what was said.

Pasted text lets you drop in anything you have copied: email threads, chat logs, interview notes, or paragraphs from a book you cannot upload as a file.

Each notebook holds multiple sources, and NotebookLM can reason across all of them at once. That cross-source ability is what makes it powerful for research and study. You are not limited to asking about one document at a time.

What makes NotebookLM different from ChatGPT

This is the question people ask most often, and the answer is simpler than you might expect. ChatGPT knows a little about almost everything because it was trained on a huge slice of the internet. NotebookLM knows a lot about the specific documents you gave it and nothing else.

That difference matters in practice. When you ask ChatGPT about a topic, it might give you a confident answer that mixes real facts with invented details. When you ask NotebookLM, it searches only your uploaded sources and tells you exactly which page or paragraph it found the answer on.

Citations are the feature that changes how you trust the output. Click a citation number and NotebookLM highlights the relevant passage in your original source. You can verify every claim in seconds. For students writing papers, researchers reviewing literature, or professionals preparing reports, that verification step is not optional. It is essential.

NotebookLM also excels at working across multiple documents simultaneously. Upload five research papers and ask "Where do these authors agree and disagree?" ChatGPT cannot do that with your specific PDFs unless you paste them in one at a time. NotebookLM handles the whole set natively.

How to get started today

You do not need to install anything or sign up for a waitlist. NotebookLM is available right now in your browser. Here is the fastest path to your first notebook:

Open notebooklm.google.com in Chrome or any modern browser. Sign in with the Google account you already use for Gmail or Drive. Click "Create new notebook" and name it something you will recognize later.

Add one source to start. If you are a student, upload a lecture slide PDF. If you are researching a topic, paste a link to a good article. If you are just experimenting, paste a YouTube URL for a video you have been meaning to watch.

Once the source finishes processing (usually under a minute), type a question in the chat box. Try "Give me a summary in five bullet points" or "What are the three most important ideas here?" Read the answer, click a citation, and see how it links back to your source.

FAQ

What is NotebookLM?
NotebookLM is a free AI research notebook from Google. You upload your own sources, then chat with an AI that answers only from those materials and cites where each answer came from.

Is Google NotebookLM free?
Yes. NotebookLM is free with a Google account. You can create notebooks, add sources, chat, and generate Audio Overviews without a paid subscription.

What can you add to NotebookLM?
PDFs, Google Docs, websites, YouTube videos, and pasted text. You can combine multiple source types in a single notebook and ask questions across all of them.

How is NotebookLM different from ChatGPT?
ChatGPT draws on broad training data and can invent facts. NotebookLM is grounded in the sources you upload and cites the exact passage for every answer, so you can verify everything yourself.
